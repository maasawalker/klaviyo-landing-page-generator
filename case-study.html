The problem

Most job postings are written for compliance, not candidates.

At Klaviyo, we have an exceptional employer brand story — the culture, the scale, the product, the people. But like most companies, that story rarely made it into the actual job posting. Candidates landed on Greenhouse listings that read like legal documents: walls of bullet points, corporate filler, zero personality.

The real gap isn't polish, it's personalization. Candidates want to feel like the role was written for them. And a great recruiting page does something equally important: it filters out the wrong people. When culture and EVP runs through every section, the wrong fits self-select out. Fewer applications mean better ones.

My goal: build a tool that closes that gap at scale, without requiring a designer, a copywriter, or three rounds of approval.

My approach

AI as a starting point. Human judgment for everything that matters.

I used Claude to build a fully branded recruiting landing page for a live Klaviyo role (Senior AI Engineer), then turned it into a generator any recruiter could use. At every step, I made deliberate decisions about what to hand to AI and what to keep human — because the line between helpful automation and brand damage is thin.

"AI is good at structure and speed. It's bad at judgment. I built the tool so AI handles the 80% that's formulaic, and flags everything it touched for human review."

Key strategic decisions

What I decided — and why it mattered.

Candidate-first copy, always
The AI was instructed to write hero headlines that speak to what the candidate gets to build or impact — not the company's needs. "Build the AI that runs itself." instead of "Senior AI Engineer." The rule was simple: if a candidate couldn't see themselves in the first sentence, the page had already failed.
→ Because candidates decide in seconds. The headline is your only shot.
Real brand assets, not approximations
I sourced the actual Klaviyo Degular font, exact hex codes, flag mark separators, and brand rules from the real 2026 brand guidelines Figma file — not a generic template. Every design decision was cross-referenced against documented brand specs (pill colors, ticker height, flag background opacity rules).
→ Because "close enough" erodes trust over time. If a recruiter generates a page that looks off-brand, the tool is worse than nothing.
Qualifications: verbatim from the JD, no exceptions
I explicitly told the AI not to summarize, rephrase, or combine requirements. If the JD listed 10 bullets, the page shows 10 bullets. This was a deliberate override of the AI's instinct to "improve" copy — because accuracy in qualifications is a legal and trust issue, not a style choice.
→ Because candidates fact-check. If the page says something different from the JD they received, you've lost credibility before the first call.
Persona context as a separate input layer
The Why Klaviyo section and CTA needed to speak differently to an engineer vs. a marketer vs. a sales candidate. I built a persona context layer — 5 pre-written EB briefs (Engineering, Marketing, Sales, Product, People) that the AI uses alongside the JD. The recruiter picks a preset; the AI adjusts its tone, language, and motivators accordingly.
→ Because "great opportunity" means something different to every candidate type. One-size-fits-all EB copy is just a different kind of wall of text.
Culture section stays human — always
The "Built Different" culture section uses Klaviyo's actual employee quotes and culture language. I excluded it from AI generation entirely — it never gets overwritten by a parse. Culture is the most human part of an employer brand, and the most easily flattened by AI trying to be helpful.
→ Because culture authenticity is the whole point. An AI-generated culture quote is worse than no quote.
Field state system: make AI oversight visible
Every field the AI touches turns yellow. Fields with template defaults are grey. Fields the recruiter has personally reviewed and edited go white. A completeness score shows exactly what's still unreviewed. The tool makes AI involvement visible and auditable rather than hiding it.
→ Because a recruiter who doesn't know what AI changed can't catch what AI got wrong. Transparency builds trust in the tool.
Social sharing: placed at peak engagement, not afterthought
Share buttons went in two places: a sticky sidebar on desktop (always visible while scrolling) and a "Know someone? Share this role — your friends deserve a job they actually rave about." strip directly after the CTA. Not in the footer where no one looks. The copy is designed to feel like a nudge from a friend, not a recruiter blast.
→ Because the moment right after someone finishes reading is when they're most likely to share. That's the peak, not the bottom of the page.
UTM tracking + analytics built in from the start
The generator produces pre-tagged UTM links for LinkedIn, email, Slack, and job boards — and embeds a GA4 snippet that fires on every "Apply now" click. I added this not because a recruiter asked for it, but because a TA director or CMO would immediately ask "how do we know this is working?" Having the answer built in changes the conversation.
→ Because any EB tool that can't prove ROI will eventually be cut. Attribution closes the loop.
Built the generator, not just the page
After building the Senior AI Engineer page, I didn't stop. I turned it into a tool any Klaviyo recruiter can use — with a form, live preview, AI parse, persona presets, and one-click deploy. The decision to build the system, not just the artifact, is the difference between a one-time project and scalable employer brand infrastructure.
→ Because the real EB problem isn't one bad job posting. It's the fact that every job posting is bad. Scale is the point.

What I learned

The most important lessons.

Constraints are a feature. Working within Klaviyo's real brand guidelines — not approximating them — forced better decisions at every step. The brand spec told me exactly what a pill should look like, what opacity a flag background should be, how tall the ticker should be. Having a system made the product feel coherent, not assembled.

AI fluency is knowing when not to use it. The tool's best decisions came from knowing what to hand to AI and what to keep human. AI can write a hero headline. It cannot decide whether that headline is true to a company's culture. That line matters.

The product story is the portfolio. This tool demonstrates product thinking, EB strategy, AI fluency, and technical execution — in one artifact. I could have just written a deck about why recruiting pages matter. I built the thing instead.
